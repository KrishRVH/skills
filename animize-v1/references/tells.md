# Tells

Use this catalog during step 2. Entries are review cues bound to the disposition system, not banned forms and not evidence of authorship. Every plausible hit gets one disposition:

- **Fix** when the passage performs unearned importance, evades a needed claim, or flattens the voice or structure. This is the default in unowned text.
- **Retain** with a stated reason: the wording is accurate and deliberate, characteristic of the writer (owned text; voice-based retention requires evidence of habit, such as a second instance or a supplied sample, because one occurrence is not a habit), required by the genre or house style, or mentioned rather than used (quotes, titles, examples under discussion).
- **Flag** when repair needs a fact, source, interpretation, or decision outside the source boundary.

Each entry's keep-when line lists its legitimate retain reasons. Tripwires are countable thresholds that force dispositions on a pattern; they never license mechanical deletion. Structural and evidentiary defects usually matter more than any single word.

Examples obey the same source boundary as real edits. When an After removes or adds an assertion, a Source line states the evidence that makes it safe; without that evidence, restate the claim plainly or flag it instead. Watched cues appear in backticks because they are mentioned here, not used. Tags name the usual failure: [performs], [evades], [flattens].

## 1. Words

### Stock figurative vocabulary [performs]

`tapestry`, `symphony`, `labyrinth`, `beacon`, `landscape`, `realm`, and `ecosystem` in abstract use decorate a claim without making it more exact, as do figurative `pivotal`, `paramount`, `testament`, `intricate`, `nuanced`, `multifaceted`, `interplay`, `transformative`, and `groundbreaking`. For an empty figurative use, state the underlying fact or remove the decoration.
Keep when: literal, technical, quoted, or genuinely characteristic of an owned voice.

> Source: The institute opened in 1989, when responsibility for regional statistics moved from the ministry to the institute.
>
> Before: "Opening the institute in 1989 marked a pivotal moment in the regional statistics landscape."
>
> After: "When the institute opened in 1989, responsibility for regional statistics moved from the ministry to the institute."

### Inflated verbs [performs]

Reflexive `delve`, `embark`, `spearhead`, `garner`, `leverage`, `utilize`, `harness`, `facilitate`, `empower`, `streamline`, `foster`, `showcase`, `underscore`, `bolster`, `elevate`, `supercharge`, and consistency cliches `align with` and `resonate with`. Prefer the concrete action already present: study, start, lead, get, use, help, simplify, show, support, match, fit, or a more exact verb.
Keep when: a formal or domain verb expresses a real distinction the plain verb loses.

> Before: "The team leveraged the index to facilitate faster searches."
>
> After: "The team used the index to speed up searches."

### Promotional adjectives [performs]

`seamless`, `effortless`, `holistic`, `comprehensive`, `cutting-edge`, `state-of-the-art`, `best-in-class`, `world-class`, `revolutionary`, `innovative`, `robust`, `dynamic`, `game-changing` substitute praise for evidence. Replace the praise with the supported mechanism, comparison, or limitation; if none is available, narrow or cut the claim.
Keep when: the term is a defined technical property (a robust estimator) or quoted.

> Source: Invalid rows are reported and the import continues.
>
> Before: "The update's robust error handling reports invalid rows and continues the import, creating a seamless experience."
>
> After: "The update reports invalid rows and continues the import."

### Scenic and heritage puffery [performs]

Travel-brochure phrasing: `nestled`, `in the heart of`, `rich history`, `rich cultural heritage`, `breathtaking`, `stunning natural beauty`, `vibrant`, `hidden gem`, `must-see`, `must-visit`, `diverse array`, `captivates residents and visitors alike`. In factual prose, keep the location or observable feature and remove the applause.
Keep when: the register is itself promotional by request, or the wording is quoted.

> Source: Alamata Raya Kobo is a town in Ethiopia's Gonder region.
>
> Before: "Nestled within the breathtaking Gonder region, Alamata Raya Kobo stands as a vibrant town with a rich cultural heritage."
>
> After: "Alamata Raya Kobo is a town in Ethiopia's Gonder region."

### Business shorthand [evades]

`navigate challenges`, `lean into`, `deep dive`, `double down`, `circle back`, `touch base`, `move the needle`, `low-hanging fruit`, `stakeholders`, `synergy` can hide the actor or the action. Name the people, decision, work, or measurable change when the material does.
Keep when: the intended readers use the shorthand precisely and a replacement would be longer or less exact.

### Empty intensifiers and commentary [evades]

Apply a deletion test to `really`, `just`, `literally`, `honestly`, `genuinely`, `simply`, `actually`, `truly`, `deeply`, `fundamentally`, `incredibly`, `importantly`, `interestingly`, `undoubtedly`, `arguably`, and `quietly` used for drama. If deleting the word changes nothing, leave it out.
Keep when: the word carries the writer's actual emphasis or uncertainty: "I honestly do not know." "It tends to fail under load." "That was very close." Scrubbing every hedge produces sanitized text, which is its own defect.

### Vocabulary drift (sweep calibration only)

Overused sets co-occur and shift by era. Roughly: 2023 to mid-2024 leaned on `delve`, `tapestry`, `testament`, `intricate`, `meticulous`, `pivotal`, `underscore`, `garner`, `boasts`, `interplay`, `vibrant`, `crucial`, and sentence-initial `Additionally`; mid-2024 to mid-2025 on `align with`, `fostering`, `showcasing`, `highlighting`, `enhance`, `enduring`; mid-2025 onward on `emphasizing`, `enhance`, `highlighting`, `showcasing`, plus the notability phrases of §6. These words cluster: where there is one, expect others, and recent sets matter more than the famous early words. Use this for calibrating the sweep, never as an authorship or model attribution.

## 2. Phrases

### Ceremonial openers [performs]

`Here's the thing`, `Let me be clear`, `The truth is`, `The uncomfortable truth is`, `To be honest`, `It turns out`, `Let's dive in`, `Let's unpack this`, `Without further ado`, `What if I told you`, `Can we talk about`, `Real talk`, and standalone theatrical `Honestly?` or `Look,` before an ordinary point. Cut the runway when the next sentence carries the whole point.
Keep when: the speaker's manner or a genuine conversational turn depends on it.

> Before: "Here's the thing: the pipeline fails when a row has no sample ID."
>
> After: "The pipeline fails when a row has no sample ID."

### Filler constructions [flattens]

Shorten when the repair preserves emphasis and register:

| Filler | Usual repair |
|---|---|
| `It is worth noting that` | state the point |
| `in order to` | to |
| `due to the fact that` | because |
| `at this point in time` | now |
| `in the event that` | if |
| `has the ability to` | can |
| `made a decision` | decided |
| `with regard to` / `in terms of` | name the subject |
| `at the end of the day` | delete |
| `in today's world` / `in the age of` | delete |
| `the reality is` | delete |
| `a wide range of` | name supported examples or the accurate category |

Keep when: deliberate legal wording, rhythmic repetition, or a voice-consistent phrase; brevity alone is not a reason.

### Emphasis instructions [performs]

`Full stop`, `Period`, `Let that sink in`, `Read that again`, `Make no mistake`, `This matters because` command a reaction the evidence has not earned. Let the claim and its evidence create the emphasis.

### Faux-insight framing [performs]

`What most people get wrong`, `What nobody tells you`, `The part everyone misses`, `The real question is`, `At its core`, `What really matters`, `The deeper issue` present an ordinary claim as a revelation. Remove the frame or support its scope.

> Source: Distribution determines whether people encounter the product.
>
> Before: "The part everyone misses is that distribution determines whether people encounter the product."
>
> After: "Distribution determines whether people encounter the product."

### Importance inflation [performs]

`stands as a testament`, `marks a pivotal moment`, `plays a vital role`, `underscores its importance`, `highlights its significance`, `reflects broader trends`, `cements its position`, `leaves a lasting legacy`, `indelible mark`, `key turning point`, `watershed moment`, `deeply rooted`, `setting the stage for`. State the event and the supported consequence; the reader judges importance.
Keep when: the significance claim is substance in an opinion register, in which case restate it plainly as the writer's evaluation, or it is sourced.

> Source: The program moved applications online in 2021 and stopped accepting paper applications.
>
> Before: "Moving applications online in 2021 marked a pivotal moment for the program."
>
> After: "The program moved applications online in 2021 and stopped accepting paper applications."

> Source: The author's own post; the evaluation is theirs to make.
>
> Before: "...marking a pivotal shift in how teams navigate upgrades."
>
> After: "...and I think it changes how teams handle upgrades."

### Correspondence and process residue [evades]

Remove accidental conversational wrappers and document self-narration: `Certainly`, `Of course!`, `Great question`, `I hope this helps`, `Would you like me to`, `Let me know if`, `Here is an overview`, `Here's a template for your`, `In this section we will`, `As we will see`, `Let me walk you through`. Flag unresolved placeholders (`[Your Name]`, `[insert source]`, `PASTE_URL_HERE`, `2025-XX-XX`, internal drafting instructions).
Keep when: tokens are intentional in a template or code.

> Before: "Here is an overview of the French Revolution. I hope this helps! Let me know if you'd like me to expand any section."
>
> After: "The French Revolution began in 1789 when financial crisis and food shortages led to widespread unrest."

### Model self-reference and cutoff disclaimers [evades]

`As an AI language model`, `as of my last knowledge update`, `up to my last training update`, `based on available information`, `in the provided sources`, `while specific details are limited`, and partial refusals (`I cannot X, but I can...`). Remove the wrapper and keep only content the boundary supports; if removing it leaves nothing verifiable, cut or flag the passage.

> Source: No source in scope documents the founding date.
>
> Before: "While specific details about the company's founding are not extensively documented in readily available sources, it appears to have been established sometime in the 1990s."
>
> After: "The available sources do not document the company's founding date."

### Speculation after missing information [evades]

A disclosure that records are limited does not authorize a guess about motives, upbringing, privacy, or likely actions. State the search scope accurately or omit the unsupported inference.

> Source: The archive contains no record of her early life and no evidence about her privacy preferences.
>
> Before: "The archive contains no record of her early life, suggesting that she maintains a low profile and prefers privacy."
>
> After: "The archive contains no record of her early life."

### Didactic disclaimers [performs]

`It is important to note`, `It is crucial to remember`, `worth noting`, advice to an imagined reader (`always check before you use something`), and `may vary` without the variance. State the specific caveat with its specifics attached, or cut.

> Source: Copyright terms differ by country.
>
> Before: "It's important to remember that what's free in one country might not be free in another, so always check before you use something."
>
> After: "Public-domain status varies by country."

## 3. Sentences

### Reflexive contrast formulas [performs]

`not X but Y`, `not just X, but also Y`, `It is not X. It is Y.`, `The question isn't X, it's Y`, `X isn't the problem; Y is`, `No X, no Y, just Z`, `stops being X and starts being Y`, negative listings (`Not a tool. Not a framework. A philosophy.`), reflexive `X rather than Y`, and clipped tailing negations (`..., no guessing.`). State the actual point directly; a negative list becomes its final item.
Keep when: the contrast corrects a real, named misconception, distinguishes defined terms, or is deliberate rhetoric in an owned voice.

> Before: "Only the colors changed. It is not a redesign; it is a palette update."
>
> After: "Only the colors changed."

> Before: "The options come from the selected item, no guessing."
>
> After: "The options come from the selected item, so the user never has to guess."

### Padded triads [flattens]

Three parallel adjectives, examples, clauses, or bullets can sound complete without being informative. Cut redundant items or give each necessary item its real role.
Keep when: the set is naturally three, the categories are required, or the triad is deliberate rhetoric.

> Source: The event has talks and panels, with unstructured time between sessions.
>
> Before: "The event features keynote sessions, panel discussions, and networking opportunities, promising innovation, inspiration, and industry insights."
>
> After: "The event has talks and panels, with time between sessions to meet people."

### Repeated symmetry [flattens]

Consecutive sentences with the same opening and shape make different ideas sound interchangeable. Reshape the accidental repetition; give the second thought a different length or angle.
Keep when: parallelism makes a real comparison easier to follow.
Tripwire: three consecutive sentences sharing shape and length force dispositions on the run.

> Before: "Engineers want clarity. Managers want context."
>
> After: "Engineers want clarity. For managers it is more about context, the things around the decision that you cannot see from outside."

### Self-answered rhetorical questions [performs]

A question answered in the next breath (`What does this mean? It means...`, `The result? Devastating.`) is a mechanical reveal. Fold it into a statement.
Keep when: the question improves navigation, teaching, or voice, or is genuinely left open for the reader.

> Before: "What causes the delay? The approval queue causes the delay."
>
> After: "The approval queue causes the delay."

### Colon reveals [performs]

Noun phrase, colon, dramatic payoff: `The best part: it learns.` Rewrite as a plain sentence; colons serve lists, labels, definitions, and quotes.
Keep when: the colon is a label or definition convention of the format.

> Before: "The detail that makes it work: a separate agent grades it."
>
> After: "A separate agent does the grading, which is what makes it work."

### Staccato drama [performs] [flattens]

Several short fragments in a row manufacture intensity. Combine them when the syntax fragments one thought; one short sentence may still land a point.
Keep when: the fragments reflect the speaker, the scene, or a deliberate change of pace.

> Before: "The import stopped. No warning. No recovery. The batch was gone."
>
> After: "The import stopped without warning or recovery, and the batch was gone."

### Manufactured kickers [performs]

Aphoristic closers (`X is the currency of Y`, `X is not a tool but a mirror`) and a quotable landing on every paragraph. Delete the kicker rather than improving it; end on the clearest supported fact, implication, action, or honest open point.
Keep when: an earned line genuinely belongs to the writer.

> Source: Users in the study described symmetric layouts as more predictable.
>
> Before: "Symmetry is the language of trust."
>
> After: "Users in the study found symmetric layouts more predictable."

### Trailing participle interpretations [performs] [evades]

A final `highlighting`, `underscoring`, `emphasizing`, `reflecting`, `symbolizing`, `showcasing`, `ensuring`, `demonstrating`, `fostering`, or `contributing to` clause attaches an unsupported interpretation to a fact. Cut it, source it, or state the concrete consequence.
Keep when: a named source actually made the interpretation; then attribute it.

> Source: The release adds CSV export; the source makes no claim about the team's commitment.
>
> Before: "The release adds CSV export, showcasing the team's commitment to flexible workflows."
>
> After: "The release adds CSV export."

> Source: Sources say the palette was chosen to evoke bluebonnets and the Gulf of Mexico; they claim nothing about community connection.
>
> Before: "The temple's palette of blue, green, and gold resonates with the region's natural beauty, reflecting the community's deep connection to the land."
>
> After: "The temple is painted blue, green, and gold, colors chosen to evoke Texas bluebonnets and the Gulf of Mexico."

### False ranges [performs]

`From X to Y` implies a scale or coverage relationship. When the endpoints are unrelated examples, list them directly or describe the real relationship.

> Source: The book has chapters on the Big Bang, star formation, and dark matter.
>
> Before: "The book takes readers from the singularity of the Big Bang to the enigmatic dance of dark matter."
>
> After: "The book covers the Big Bang, star formation, and dark matter."

### Inflated copulas [performs]

`serves as`, `stands as`, `functions as`, `acts as`, `operates as`, `represents`, `boasts`, `features`, `offers`, and definition-lead `refers to` inflate a simple is or has. Use the simple verb when nothing else changes; simple copulas are characteristic of clean human prose, so restore them freely.
Keep when: the stronger verb expresses a real function, role, or representation.

> Before: "Gallery 825 serves as the association's exhibition space and features four rooms."
>
> After: "Gallery 825 is the association's exhibition space and has four rooms."

> Before: "Catchment area refers to the geographic area from which a hospital draws its patients."
>
> After: "A catchment area is the area from which a hospital draws its patients."

### Obscured responsibility [evades]

Two related habits hide a knowable actor. Passive and subjectless constructions (`mistakes were made`, `the decision was reached`, `No configuration file needed`): put the actor first when the reader needs to evaluate who did what. Novel animation of process nouns (`the decision emerged`, `the culture shifted`, `a complaint becomes a fix`, `a bet lives or dies`): name the human, or use you or we when none is identified.
Keep when: the actor is unknown, irrelevant, obvious, or conventionally backgrounded by the genre (a methods section), and keep ordinary metonymy such as `the data show`, `the court held`, `the paper argues`, which is idiomatic, not evasion.

> Before: "After its review, a decision was reached to approve the budget."
>
> After: "The board approved the budget after its review."

> Before: "Within a week, the complaint becomes a fix."
>
> After: "The team fixed it within a week."

### Vague or inflated attribution [evades]

`Experts argue`, `studies show`, `industry reports suggest`, `critics say`, `widely regarded as`, one source described as several, and `such as` implying a longer list than exists. Name the source and its actual claim when available; otherwise narrow the attribution, ask for the source, or flag or cut the claim. Never invent a source; an unsupported claim gets cut or flagged, not decorated.

> Source: One supplied review calls the interface confusing; no other review is available.
>
> Before: "Reviewers found the interface difficult to use."
>
> After: "One review called the interface confusing."

### Hedge stacks [evades]

Several hedges on one proposition make its uncertainty unreadable. Keep the single hedge that most accurately calibrates the claim; a precise may is stronger than a false certainly.

> Before: "The policy could potentially possibly affect response times."
>
> After: "The policy may affect response times."

### Unsupported universals [evades]

`always`, `never`, `everyone`, `nobody` overstate what the material supports. Narrow the quantifier to the evidence.
Keep when: the source can defend the universal or the definitive claim (`was the first`, `is the only`); defensible definitive statements are legitimate and often clearer.

### Repeated question or discourse openers [flattens]

Several sentences opening with `What`, `Why`, or `How`, or several paragraphs opening with `So`, `Look`, or one recycled transition, become a template. Restructure the accidental repetitions to lead with the subject or verb; occasional use is normal.

### Concession boilerplate [performs]

`Despite these challenges, X continues to thrive` dismisses the named problems with a ceremonial recovery. State the supported problems and the supported outcome, without the turn.

> Source: The town has recurring traffic congestion and water shortages; no source assesses its overall trajectory.
>
> Before: "Despite challenges including congestion and water scarcity, the town continues to thrive as an integral part of the region's growth."
>
> After: "The town has recurring traffic congestion and water shortages."

### Predicate hyphen drift [flattens]

Compounds hyphenated uniformly even after the noun. Keep attributive hyphens (`a high-quality report`); drop predicate ones (`the report is high quality`). House style wins where it specifies otherwise.

## 4. Paragraphs and composition

### A bow on every paragraph [flattens]

Repeated miniature takeaways make a piece feel pre-resolved. Let a paragraph end when its work is done.
Keep when: the genre needs explicit takeaways (instruction, decision memos).
Tripwire: when most consecutive paragraphs close on a takeaway line, force dispositions on the run.

### Announce, explain, recap [flattens]

Introductions, topic sentences, and summaries repeating one point at three scales. Remove the layer that adds no orientation or synthesis.
Keep when: a long, technical, accessible, or decision-oriented document genuinely needs the recap.

### Generic conclusions [performs]

`In conclusion`, `In summary`, `Overall`, `Ultimately`, and upbeat send-offs (`The future looks bright`, `Exciting times lie ahead`) restate the piece without adding a decision, implication, or action. Cut empty closure and end on the last concrete point, takeaway, or action.
Keep when: the genre expects synthesis and the paragraph actually provides it.

### Template-shaped structure [flattens]

Generic headings (`Introduction`, `Background`, `Challenges and Future Outlook`, `Conclusion`), a heading over every few lines, a warm-up sentence restating its heading, and thematic breaks before every heading reveal scaffolding rather than structure. Organize around the reader's questions; write headings as specific statements; cut the warm-up line.
Keep when: an academic, legal, policy, or organizational template requires the sections.

> Before: "## Performance // Speed matters. // When users hit a slow page, they leave."
>
> After: "## Performance // When users hit a slow page, they leave."

### Synonym cycling [flattens]

Unnecessary synonyms make one subject look like several. Repeat the clearest term when consistency matters.

> Before: "The protagonist faces a choice. The central figure delays it. The hero eventually decides."
>
> After: "The protagonist delays the choice but eventually decides."

### Transition clustering [flattens]

Sentence-initial `Furthermore`, `Moreover`, `Additionally`, `Consequently`, `Subsequently`, `Notably` piled up, or any single replacement word recycled. Remove transitions the logic does not need; often the connection is implicit, or And, But, or So carries it. One `however` is normal, but in unowned text sentence-initial `Additionally` is a fix even when it stands alone.
Tripwire: more than two formal transitions in a piece, or two in adjacent sentences, forces dispositions on all of them.

### Dilution [flattens]

One point restated across sections, a metaphor extended past its work, or stacked analogies supplying borrowed authority (`Apple didn't build Uber. Facebook didn't build Spotify.`). Consolidate; one point per section, one use per metaphor, one analogy or none.
Keep when: repetition is a deliberate motif and the distinctions are real.

### Change-log language in durable documentation [evades]

Docs and comments narrating what was added or replaced instead of describing current behavior. Rewrite for the current reader.
Keep when: the document is a changelog, release note, migration guide, or historical explanation.

> Before: "This function was added to replace the previous approach of iterating all items, which caused O(n²) performance."
>
> After: "This function uses a hash map for O(1) lookups, avoiding the O(n²) cost of naive iteration."

## 5. Formatting

### Mechanical list styling [flattens]

Bold-label bullets (`- **Term:** text`), identical three-item lists, tiny tables better said as a sentence, or a heading over every paragraph package weak content as structure. Choose prose, bullets, or a table by the relationship among items.
Keep when: an established house pattern or a genuinely scannable list.

> Before: "- **User Experience:** The user experience has been significantly improved. - **Performance:** Performance has been enhanced. - **Security:** Security has been strengthened with end-to-end encryption."
>
> After: "The update improves the interface, speeds up load times, and adds end-to-end encryption."

### Abstract bullets [evades]

List items that are bare category nouns (`Enhanced productivity`, `Improved efficiency`, `Better collaboration`) carry no information. Make each item a concrete, supported claim or cut the list.

> Source: Edge-case handling ships inside the package; tests are generated by the tool.
>
> Before: "Benefits: 1. Enhanced productivity 2. Improved efficiency 3. Streamlined workflows"
>
> After: "This saves time two ways: edge cases ship inside the package instead of living in docs, and the tests are generated rather than written."

### Decorative emphasis [performs]

Repeated boldface, emoji on headings or bullets, decorative symbols, and Title Case In Every Heading add energy without information. Remove decoration that conflicts with the genre or house style; sentence-case headings by default.
Keep when: functional status symbols, requested emoji, or the publication's own heading convention.

### Dashes and punctuation [flattens]

Em dashes read two ways at once: as ordinary punctuation in an established writer's hand, and, when dense and spaced, as the most widely recognized machine mannerism of the 2023 to 2026 era, a reading that costs the caller credibility whether or not it is fair. Set policy by ownership. Owned text: preserve the writer's natural rate; intervene only when clusters blur sentences or fight house style. Unowned text: strip to near zero, replacing each dash with, in order of preference, a period, comma, colon, or parentheses, and keep one only where it clearly beats all four.
A caller's request to de-AI or humanize owned text moves it to the unowned policy for exactly this pattern. Never count en dashes (ranges and relationships have their own style rules) with em dashes, and never touch dashes in code, citations, or source formats that render double hyphens.
Tripwire: two or more em dashes in one paragraph of unowned text, spaced or not, forces the fix.

> Before: "The new policy — announced without warning — affects thousands of workers."
>
> After: "The new policy, announced without warning, affects thousands of workers."

### Format leakage [evades]

Markdown in a plain-text destination, skipped heading levels, broken list nesting, raw template syntax, internal reference tokens, and characters that will not render as intended. Convert to the destination's native format; flag what you cannot convert.
Keep when: the syntax is literal content in code or examples.

### Inconsistent quotation marks [flattens]

Mixed straight and curly quotes or apostrophes in one text are usually accidental. Normalize only when the destination or house style requires it; either style alone says nothing about authorship.

## 6. Evidence and citations

### Vague declaratives [evades]

`The implications are significant`, `The reasons are structural`, `The stakes are high`, `This is genuinely hard` announce a conclusion without giving it. Name the implication, reason, or stake from the authorized material, or cut or flag the sentence.

> Source: The change requires re-registering every webhook by March.
>
> Before: "The implications for integrators are significant."
>
> After: "Integrators must re-register every webhook by March."

### Unsupported specificity and smoothing [evades]

Two failures, one boundary. Smoothing erases a supplied specific into a category; protect supplied specifics absolutely. Inventing repairs vagueness with a number, actor, mechanism, place, example, or anecdote the boundary lacks; never do it. Specificity improves prose only when it is true.

> Before: "The campaign faced financial challenges."
>
> Safe repair without more evidence: "The campaign had financial problems."
>
> Better repair: ask for or locate the authorized figure, then state it ("The campaign lost $40k in Q2").

### Unsupported interpretation or causation [evades]

Facts placed side by side can imply a cause, motive, trend, or symbolic meaning the source never establishes. Separate the facts, qualify the relationship, cite the interpretation, or flag it.

### Notability piling [performs]

Parades of publications, awards, clients, follower counts, `independent coverage`, `trade publications`, `profiled in multiple high-quality outlets`, `maintains an active social media presence`. Keep the evidence that matters to the claim, with its context; cut the parade. Do not trim a required bibliography, and do not invent context to dress up what remains.

> Source: The supplied material quotes her New York Times and BBC citations; the rest is an unsourced list.
>
> Before: "Her views have been cited in The New York Times, BBC, Financial Times, and The Hindu, and she maintains an active social media presence with over 500,000 followers."
>
> After: "Her views have been cited in The New York Times and the BBC."

### Citation integrity [evades]

Look for malformed references, impossible metadata, unresolved placeholders (`2025-XX-XX`), dead or suspicious links, missing locators where the genre requires them, tracking parameters (`utm_source=chatgpt.com`), and leaked internal citation tokens (`oaicite`, `turn0search`, `contentReference`, `[cite: 1]`, `grok_card`). When verification is in scope, check each concern against authorized sources and record uncertainty. When it is not, preserve the citation and link target and flag the concern. Never fabricate or silently substitute a reference.

## 7. Tone

### Sycophancy and announcement theater [performs]

`Great question`, `You're absolutely right`, `We're thrilled to announce`, and exclamation clusters praise the reader or the event instead of delivering information. Lead with the answer or the announcement.
Keep when: warmth fits the speaker and relationship.

> Before: "We're excited to announce a revolutionary new approach to migrations!"
>
> After: "We built the tool because hand migrations kept breaking."

### Press-release voice [performs]

Commitments to excellence, innovation, sustainability, or community conceal what an organization did. State the supported action, target, result, or limitation.
Keep when: reporting attributed language an organization actually used; then attribute it.

### Symmetrical neutrality [evades]

Perfectly balanced treatment can hide the conclusion the material supports. State the warranted position and represent genuine disagreement accurately, with its holders named. Do not manufacture certainty or erase counterevidence.

### Patronizing instruction [performs]

`Think of it as`, `Let's break this down`, and beginner metaphors talk below the intended reader. Teach at the level the audience needs.

### Unmarked coinages [performs]

A fresh label presented as established (`the supervision paradox`) manufactures authority. Describe the idea plainly, or introduce the coinage once as the writer's shorthand and define it.

### Borrowed intimacy [performs]

`Trust me`, `I promise`, `We've all been there`, drama verbs like `creeps in`, and invented personal anecdotes claim a relationship or experience the material has not earned. Cut them.
Keep when: authentic to the writer and relevant.

## 8. Audit guidance

An audit identifies observable choices and their reader effects. It does not determine whether a person or a system wrote the text.

### Report material issues

Use this shape:

> **Excerpt or location:** brief enough to identify the passage
>
> **Pattern:** the catalog name or a plain description
>
> **Effect:** what it obscures, inflates, repeats, or disrupts here
>
> **Repair:** the smallest useful direction

Group repeated instances of one pattern when a line-by-line list would be noisy, and distinguish evidence and meaning defects from minor style choices.

### Describe clusters without a verdict

Several independent problems co-occurring (unsupported importance, repetitive transitions, template structure, vague attribution) justify recommending a broader editing pass. Describe the cluster and its effect on the reader. Do not convert it into an origin claim, a probability, or an accusation, and give no advice about evading detectors.

### Do not use these as provenance evidence

Report them only when they materially harm the prose; alone they support no authorship claim: polished grammar or clean formatting; formal, academic, technical, or multilingual diction; mixed registers in multi-author or specialist documents; one transition, dash, triad, passive sentence, fragment, or rhetorical question; blandness, verbosity, or missing citations; any single word from a watch list.

### Preserve voice evidence

Characteristic vocabulary, defensible certainty, honest hedges, parentheticals, in-jokes, uneven rhythm, recurring syntax, era-bound references, and unusual concrete detail are reasons for editing restraint, not proof of who wrote the text.

If the caller needs provenance, point to process evidence: drafts, revision history, notes, source trails, timestamps, and a conversation with the writer (a writer who can explain their own choices is strong evidence; text verifiably written before November 2022 predates public chatbots entirely). Style analysis may guide those questions; it cannot settle authorship by itself.

## Evidence basis

The audit stance reflects both the limits and the bounded value of style analysis. The source taxonomy describes its indicators as descriptive, not prescriptive, and warns that their presence does not establish authorship ([Wikipedia, "Signs of AI writing," pinned revision](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing&oldid=1365519760)). Published evaluations find text-only detectors produce substantial false positives and degrade across populations, domains, generators, and edits ([Liang et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10382961/), [Weber-Wulff et al.](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40979-023-00146-z), [Dugan et al.](https://aclanthology.org/2024.acl-long.674/), [Sadasivan et al.](https://openreview.net/forum?id=OOgsAZdFOt)). One bounded study found experienced readers combined lexical and structural cues effectively on its English nonfiction sample, which supports careful review, not universal attribution ([Russell et al.](https://aclanthology.org/2025.acl-long.267/)).
