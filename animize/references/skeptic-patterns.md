# Skeptical-reader patterns

These are diagnostic clusters, not banned words and not evidence of authorship.
A skeptical reader usually reacts to several mutually reinforcing choices, not
to one dash, transition, adjective, or sentence length.

For each plausible cluster, choose `fix`, `retain`, or `flag`.

- **Fix** when it makes the piece generic, evasive, inflated, repetitive, or
  poorly fitted to the reader.
- **Retain** when it is accurate, useful, genre-required, or deliberately
  rhetorical.
- **Flag** when repair requires missing facts, sources, interpretation, or a
  caller decision.

## 1. Generic runway

**Cues:** broad statements about a changing world, declarations that a topic is
important, promises to explore or unpack it, ceremonial openers, or an overview
that repeats the title.

**Effect:** delays the actual point and makes the piece interchangeable.

**Repair:** begin with the finding, tension, problem, request, scene, or concrete
fact the genre needs.

## 2. Generic closure

**Cues:** `in conclusion`, `ultimately`, a complete recap, an optimistic
send-off, a moral, or a slogan that adds no decision or implication.

**Effect:** makes closure feel manufactured.

**Repair:** end on the last substantive consequence, action, decision, or open
question.

## 3. Exposed scaffolding

**Cues:** introduction/background/challenges/future headings without a real
need; heading over every paragraph; announce-explain-recap at several scales;
meta-language about what the section will discuss.

**Effect:** the template remains visible after drafting.

**Repair:** organize around the reader's actual questions and remove redundant
orientation.

## 4. Exhaustive symmetry

**Cues:** equal-length sections, identical paragraph shapes, repeated three-part
lists, perfectly balanced advantages and disadvantages, or every issue paired
with a generic solution.

**Effect:** different ideas receive artificial equivalence and the writer's
priorities disappear.

**Repair:** allocate space according to consequence; preserve parallelism only
when it clarifies a real comparison.

## 5. Pseudo-insight

**Cues:** `what most people miss`, `the deeper issue`, `at its core`, `the real
question`, self-answered rhetorical questions, or `not X but Y` used to stage
an ordinary distinction as revelation.

**Effect:** performs discovery instead of providing it.

**Repair:** state the distinction directly or supply the evidence that makes it
non-obvious.

## 6. Importance without consequence

**Cues:** pivotal, transformative, groundbreaking, crucial, significant,
underscores the importance, reflects a broader trend, or similar evaluation
without a named result.

**Effect:** asks the reader to feel weight that the prose has not established.

**Repair:** state the event and supported consequence; retain the evaluation
only when the source defends it.

## 7. Vague authority

**Cues:** experts say, research shows, critics argue, widely regarded, or one
source generalized into a group.

**Effect:** borrows credibility while hiding provenance and scope.

**Repair:** name the source and actual claim, narrow the attribution, or flag
missing support.

## 8. Polished abstraction

**Cues:** long runs of abstract nouns, business shorthand, promotional
adjectives, generic mechanisms, or sentences that sound complete but contain no
actor, condition, example, number, or consequence.

**Effect:** creates fluency without information.

**Repair:** use supported concrete nouns and verbs; cut the sentence if no
specific contribution remains.

## 9. Transition over-control

**Cues:** furthermore, moreover, additionally, consequently, notably, however,
or replacement transitions at the start of many sentences; explanations of
relationships already evident from adjacency.

**Effect:** makes the logic feel assembled and over-signposted.

**Repair:** remove unnecessary transitions. Keep one only when it identifies a
real relationship such as contrast, cause, sequence, or exception.

## 10. Repeated sentence machinery

**Cues:** several sentences with the same opening, clause shape, rhetorical
contrast, participial ending, colon reveal, fragment sequence, or concluding
kicker.

**Effect:** the reader hears the generator or editor's habit rather than the
subject.

**Repair:** rebuild the affected sentences around their different logical
jobs. Do not vary them randomly.

## 11. Over-complete explanation

**Cues:** every inference is stated, every example is followed by its lesson,
every paragraph ends with a takeaway, and obvious connections are narrated.

**Effect:** talks past the intended reader and removes natural compression.

**Repair:** trust the reader where the evidence and sequence already make the
point.

## 12. Synthetic balance and hedge stacks

**Cues:** multiple hedges on one claim, a concession in every paragraph,
mechanical `on one hand/on the other hand`, or a conclusion softened until no
position remains.

**Effect:** evades the level of certainty and judgment the source supports.

**Repair:** keep the single qualifier that accurately calibrates the claim and
state the warranted conclusion.

## 13. Borrowed intimacy or persona

**Cues:** `trust me`, `we have all been there`, invented confessions, casual
slang unrelated to the speaker, fake self-corrections, or unexplained emotional
claims.

**Effect:** claims a relationship or experience not established by the source.

**Repair:** remove it. Personal texture is permitted only when supplied or
explicitly authorized in fiction.

## 14. Promotional and press-release voice

**Cues:** seamless, robust, best-in-class, innovative, commitment to excellence,
we are thrilled, empowers users, or features presented as benefits without a
mechanism.

**Effect:** replaces verifiable information with applause.

**Repair:** state the feature, mechanism, comparison, target, result, or limit.

## 15. False specificity and unsupported smoothing

**Cues:** a vague source rewritten with an invented number or example; a precise
source rewritten into a broad category; adjacent facts turned into cause,
motive, trend, or symbolism.

**Effect:** changes substance while appearing to improve prose.

**Repair:** restore the source boundary. Ask for evidence, qualify, separate the
facts, or flag the gap.

## 16. Session and format residue

**Cues:** `certainly`, `great question`, `here is an overview`, offers to do more,
internal instructions, unresolved placeholders, leaked reference tokens,
Markdown in a plain-text destination, or broken list structure.

**Effect:** exposes the generation or drafting environment and can corrupt the
artifact.

**Repair:** remove accidental wrappers after preserving any substantive
limitation; convert formatting only when the destination is known.

## 17. Mechanical lexical cleanup

**Cues:** every formal word replaced with a shorter one, all dashes removed,
all passive voice made active, all transitions deleted, or watched words
systematically substituted.

**Effect:** creates a second artificial house style and may damage precision.

**Repair:** restore forms that are accurate, idiomatic, genre-native, or
necessary. Fix the underlying issue, not the token.

## Cluster rule

One cue rarely matters. Intervene aggressively when independent defects cluster,
for example:

- generic opening + symmetrical sections + transition repetition + generic
  conclusion
- vague authority + importance inflation + unsupported causal interpretation
- polished abstraction + no concrete actor + exhaustive coverage
- rhetorical contrasts + slogan endings + borrowed intimacy

When a cluster exists under `rewrite_depth: recompose`, rebuild the affected
section from the content lock instead of patching each sentence.
