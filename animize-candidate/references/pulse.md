# Pulse

Use this reference during Step 3 for Edit and Write jobs. The aim is not to add
quirks. It is to keep or recover the choices that make this particular writer
clear and recognizable.

The precedence in [SKILL.md](../SKILL.md#two-laws) governs every move. Facts and
meaning come before house style, house style before established voice, and
voice before these heuristics.

## Preserve a voice before shaping one

For an edit, reread the voice signals from Step 1. Notice characteristic
vocabulary, sentence movement, directness, humor, uncertainty, digressions,
technical density, and level of polish. Change only what impedes the piece.
The writer should still recognize their own decisions.

For a new draft, use samples when the caller supplies them. Match durable
features such as register, density, directness, and paragraph movement. Do not
copy memorable phrases or exaggerate superficial quirks. If the caller gives
no sample or named style, default to a clear, unshowy voice.

These loose profiles can help interpret a brief; they are starting points, not
templates. Use a named tone at the lightest intensity that makes it
recognizable. Do not turn one quality, such as sarcasm or warmth, into a
recurring device.

- **Clear and analytical:** follow the argument, prefer concrete verbs, and
  state the position without decorative emphasis.
- **Casual and narrative:** allow contractions, direct address, asides, and
  scene-level detail already supported by the material.
- **Sharp and opinionated:** put the judgment early, show the evidence, and
  preserve honest concessions.
- **Warm and professional:** use courteous, direct sentences without corporate
  ceremony or forced familiarity.

## Let rhythm follow thought

Read paragraphs as units. Revise cadence when repeated sentence lengths,
openings, or clause shapes make the argument feel mechanical. A short sentence
may land a point; a long one may hold a qualification or sequence. Neither is
required.

Do not alternate lengths by formula, add fragments for texture, or vary a
construction that serves parallel ideas. Deliberate repetition can clarify an
argument or create emphasis.

Read the result internally at normal speed. Repair the places where the syntax
obscures the thought, the pace stalls, or several sentences strike the same
note by accident.

## Earn specificity

Prefer concrete actors, actions, mechanisms, examples, names, numbers, dates,
and places when the authorized material provides them and the reader needs
them. Keep the broader claim when greater precision would be false or
irrelevant.

Use the anywhere test on important sentences: if a sentence could appear
unchanged in an unrelated piece, ask what it contributes here. Replace it with
supported detail, narrow it to the real claim, or cut it.

State limitations at the point where they change the reader's decision. Do not
add a disclaimer merely to sound candid.

## Make stance proportionate to evidence

State the position the material supports. Remove stacked hedges that obscure
it, but preserve uncertainty that changes the claim. A precise `may` is often
stronger than an unjustified certainty.

Name the actor when responsibility matters and the material identifies one.
Passive voice is useful when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, already clear,
or deliberately backgrounded by the genre.

Keep defensible superlatives and comparisons only when the source boundary
supports them and the genre benefits from them. They are claims, not signals of
a human voice.

## Open and end for the genre

An opening should perform the genre's first job:

- an essay or post can lead with the tension, judgment, scene, or strongest
  concrete fact
- a report or decision memo should lead with the finding, decision, or request
- technical documentation should orient the reader to the task and
  prerequisites
- an abstract should summarize the question, method, result, and significance
  in the field's expected form

Cut generic setup that delays that job. Keep background the reader genuinely
needs.

An ending may give a conclusion, decision, next action, implication, or honest
open question. Avoid repeating the whole piece or attaching an upbeat send-off
without new information. Keep recaps when the genre, length, or accessibility
needs one.

## Preserve texture; do not fabricate it

Keep existing parentheticals, self-corrections, asymmetry, unusual details, and
unresolved feelings when they serve the writer. Do not add them as proof of
humanity. Clean neutral prose can be the right outcome.

The invisible-edit test is simple: the prose should feel more exact and easier
to follow without drawing attention to the editor.

## Register guide

| Register                   | Favor                                                                                                 | Watch for                                                                                           |
| -------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Scientific or academic     | field terminology, calibrated claims, expected section structure, and the field's citation style      | injected personality, oversimplification, or deleting necessary passive constructions and summaries |
| Technical                  | peer-level explanation, examples, edge cases, expected output, and explicit limitations               | tutorial patter, marketing claims, or unexplained abstraction                                       |
| Essay, blog, or newsletter | a visible point of view, concrete scenes or examples from the source, and flexible paragraph movement | lesson-shaped endings, borrowed intimacy, or narrator-at-a-distance generalities                    |
| Professional post          | an informative opening, one coherent idea, readable line breaks, and a concrete close                 | engagement bait, slogan stacks, or a moral not earned by the material                               |
| Marketing or product       | verifiable claims, named comparisons, useful limits, and language the audience uses                   | uncheckable superlatives, ceremonial announcements, or feature lists presented as outcomes          |
| Legal or policy            | defined terms, exact scope, consistent modality, and required formality                               | edits that change obligations, exceptions, citations, or deliberate repetition                      |
| Fiction or creative        | the narrator's and characters' distinct voices, purposeful rhythm, and detail allowed by the brief    | normalizing every voice or mistaking stylization for a defect                                       |

## Final read-back

Ask four questions:

1. Does each substantive change preserve or clarify what the source supports?
2. Does the result still sound like this writer or the requested voice?
3. Does the form suit this reader and genre?
4. Did any repair become a new repeated mannerism?
