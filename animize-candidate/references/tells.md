# Tells

Use this catalog during Step 2. Its entries are review cues, not banned forms
and not evidence of authorship. A cue matters only when it exposes a real
reader-facing problem in context.

For each plausible hit, choose one disposition:

- **Fix** when the passage performs importance, evades a needed claim, or
  flattens the writer's voice or structure.
- **Retain** when the wording is accurate, idiomatic, deliberate, required by
  the genre or house style, or characteristic of the writer.
- **Flag** when repair needs a fact, source, interpretation, or decision outside
  the authorized material.

The tags identify the usual failure: **[performs]**, **[evades]**, or
**[flattens]**. Structural and evidentiary defects usually matter more than an
isolated word.

Before-and-After examples obey the same source boundary as real edits. When an
After removes an assertion, a `Source` line states the evidence boundary that
makes the removal safe. Without that evidence, preserve or flag the assertion.

## 1. Words

### Stock figurative vocabulary [performs]

Words such as `tapestry`, `symphony`, `labyrinth`, `beacon`, `landscape`,
`realm`, and `ecosystem` can decorate an abstract claim without making it more
exact. Figurative uses of `pivotal`, `testament`, `intricate`, `nuanced`,
`multifaceted`, `interplay`, `transformative`, and `groundbreaking` can do the
same.

Do not replace a word merely because it appears here. Ask what work it does.
Keep literal, technical, quoted, or voice-consistent uses. For an empty
figurative use, state the underlying fact or remove the decoration.

> Source: The institute opened in 1989, when responsibility for regional
> statistics moved from the ministry to the institute.
>
> Before: "Opening the institute in 1989 marked a pivotal moment in regional
> statistics."
>
> After: "When the institute opened in 1989, responsibility for regional
> statistics moved from the ministry to the institute."

### Inflated verbs [performs]

Review reflexive uses of `delve`, `embark`, `spearhead`, `garner`, `leverage`,
`utilize`, `harness`, `facilitate`, `empower`, `streamline`, `foster`,
`showcase`, `underscore`, `bolster`, `elevate`, and `supercharge`.

Prefer the concrete action already present: `study`, `start`, `lead`, `get`,
`use`, `help`, `simplify`, `show`, `support`, or a more exact verb. Keep a
formal or domain-specific verb when it expresses a real distinction.

> Before: "The team leveraged the index to facilitate faster searches."
>
> After: "The team used the index to speed up searches."

### Promotional adjectives [performs]

`Seamless`, `effortless`, `holistic`, `comprehensive`, `cutting-edge`,
`state-of-the-art`, `best-in-class`, `world-class`, `innovative`, `robust`,
`dynamic`, and `game-changing` often substitute praise for evidence. Replace
the praise with the supported mechanism, comparison, or limitation. If none is
available, narrow or cut the claim.

> Source: Invalid rows are reported and the import continues.
>
> Before: "The update's robust error handling reports invalid rows and
> continues the import, creating a seamless experience."
>
> After: "The update reports invalid rows and continues the import."

### Scenic and heritage puffery [performs]

Review travel-brochure phrases such as `nestled`, `in the heart of`, `rich
history`, `rich cultural heritage`, `breathtaking`, `stunning natural beauty`,
`vibrant`, `hidden gem`, `must-see`, `diverse array`, and `captivates residents
and visitors alike`. In factual prose, keep the location or observable feature
and remove the applause.

> Source: Alamata Raya Kobo is a town in Ethiopia's Gonder region.
>
> Before: "Nestled in the Gonder region of Ethiopia, Alamata Raya Kobo is a
> vibrant town."
>
> After: "Alamata Raya Kobo is a town in Ethiopia's Gonder region."

### Business shorthand [evades]

Phrases such as `navigate challenges`, `lean into`, `deep dive`, `double down`,
`circle back`, `touch base`, `move the needle`, `low-hanging fruit`,
`stakeholders`, and `synergy` can hide an actor or action. Name the people,
decision, work, or measurable change when the material does.

Keep established shorthand when the intended readers use it precisely and a
replacement would be longer or less exact.

### Empty intensifiers and commentary [evades]

Apply a deletion test to `really`, `just`, `literally`, `actually`, `truly`,
`deeply`, `fundamentally`, `incredibly`, `importantly`, `interestingly`,
`undoubtedly`, `arguably`, and similar commentary. If deleting the word changes
nothing, leave it out.

Preserve an intensifier or hedge that carries the writer's actual emphasis or
uncertainty:

- "I honestly do not know."
- "It tends to fail under load."
- "That was very close."

## 2. Phrases

### Ceremonial openers [performs]

Review `Here's the thing`, `Let me be clear`, `The truth is`, `It turns out`,
`Let's dive in`, `Without further ado`, `What if I told you`, `Real talk`, and
similar runway. Cut the setup when the next sentence carries the whole point.
Keep it when the speaker's manner or the conversational turn genuinely depends
on it.

> Before: "Here's the thing: the pipeline fails when a row has no sample ID."
>
> After: "The pipeline fails when a row has no sample ID."

### Filler constructions [flattens]

Shorten a construction when doing so preserves its emphasis and register:

| Filler                    | Usual repair                                         |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| `It is worth noting that` | state the point                                      |
| `in order to`             | `to`                                                 |
| `due to the fact that`    | `because`                                            |
| `at this point in time`   | `now`                                                |
| `in the event that`       | `if`                                                 |
| `has the ability to`      | `can`                                                |
| `made a decision`         | `decided`                                            |
| `with regard to`          | name the subject                                     |
| `a wide range of`         | name supported examples or use the accurate category |

Do not compress deliberate legal wording, rhythmic repetition, or a
voice-consistent phrase solely for brevity.

### Emphasis instructions [performs]

`Full stop`, `Period`, `Let that sink in`, `Read that again`, `Make no mistake`,
and `This matters because` often command a reaction the evidence has not
earned. Let the claim and evidence create the emphasis.

### Faux-insight framing [performs]

`What most people get wrong`, `What nobody tells you`, `The part everyone
misses`, `The real question`, `At its core`, and `The deeper issue` can present
an ordinary claim as a revelation. Remove the frame or support its scope.

> Source: Distribution determines whether people encounter the product.
>
> Before: "The part everyone misses is that distribution determines whether
> people encounter the product."
>
> After: "Distribution determines whether people encounter the product."

### Importance inflation [performs]

Review `marks a pivotal moment`, `plays a vital role`, `underscores its
importance`, `reflects broader trends`, `cements its position`, `leaves a
lasting legacy`, `key turning point`, `setting the stage for`, and similar
claims. State the event and the supported consequence. Let the reader judge its
importance.

> Source: The program moved applications online in 2021 and stopped accepting
> paper applications.
>
> Before: "Moving applications online in 2021 marked a pivotal moment for the
> program."
>
> After: "The program moved applications online in 2021 and stopped accepting
> paper applications."

### Correspondence and process residue [evades]

Remove accidental conversational wrappers and document self-narration:
`Certainly`, `Great question`, `I hope this helps`, `Would you like me to`,
`Let me know if`, `Here is an overview`, `In this section we will`, and `As we
will see`.

Flag unresolved placeholders such as `[Your Name]`, `[insert source]`,
`PASTE_URL_HERE`, incomplete dates, or internal drafting instructions. Preserve
intentional tokens in templates and code.

### Speculation after missing information [evades]

A disclosure such as "records are limited" does not authorize a guess about a
person's motives, upbringing, privacy, or likely actions. State the search
scope accurately or omit the unsupported inference.

> Source: The archive contains no record of her early life and provides no
> evidence about her privacy preferences.
>
> Before: "The archive contains no record of her early life, suggesting that
> she preferred privacy."
>
> After: "The archive contains no record of her early life."

## 3. Sentences

### Reflexive contrast formulas [performs]

Repeated `not X but Y`, `not only X`, `It is not X. It is Y.`, `No X, no Y,
just Z`, and `stops being X and starts being Y` can manufacture revelation.
State the actual comparison directly.

Keep a contrast when it corrects a real misconception, distinguishes terms,
or creates intentional rhetoric.

> Before: "Only the colors changed. It is not a redesign; it is a palette
> update."
>
> After: "Only the colors changed."

### Padded triads [flattens]

Three parallel adjectives, examples, clauses, or bullets can sound complete
without being informative. Cut redundant items or give each necessary item its
real role. Keep natural sets of three, required categories, and deliberate
rhetorical triads.

### Repeated symmetry [flattens]

Several consecutive sentences with the same opening and shape can make
different ideas sound interchangeable. Reshape a sentence when the repetition
is accidental. Keep parallelism that makes a comparison easier to follow.

### Self-answered rhetorical questions [performs]

A question followed immediately by its own answer can become a mechanical
reveal. Fold it into a statement unless the question improves navigation,
teaching, or voice.

> Before: "What causes the delay? The approval queue causes the delay."
>
> After: "The approval queue causes the delay."

### Staccato drama [performs] [flattens]

Several short fragments in a row can manufacture intensity. Combine them when
the syntax fragments one thought. Keep short sentences that reflect the
speaker, the scene, or a deliberate change in pace.

> Before: "The import stopped. No warning. No recovery. The batch was gone."
>
> After: "The import stopped without a warning or recovery, and the batch was
> gone."

### Manufactured kickers [performs]

Review aphoristic endings such as `X is the currency of Y`, `X is not a tool
but a mirror`, or a slogan appended to every paragraph. End on the clearest
fact, implication, action, or honest unresolved point already supported by the
piece. Keep an earned line that belongs to the writer.

### Trailing participle interpretations [performs] [evades]

A final `highlighting`, `underscoring`, `reflecting`, `symbolizing`,
`showcasing`, `ensuring`, or `demonstrating` clause may attach an unsupported
interpretation to a fact. Cut it, source it, or state the concrete consequence.

> Source: The release adds CSV export; the source makes no claim about the
> team's commitment.
>
> Before: "The release adds CSV export, showcasing the team's commitment to
> flexible workflows."
>
> After: "The release adds CSV export."

### False ranges [performs]

`From X to Y` implies a scale, progression, or coverage relationship. When the
endpoints are merely unrelated examples, use a direct list or describe the
actual relationship.

> Source: The book has one chapter on the Big Bang and one on dark matter.
>
> Before: "The book covers subjects from the Big Bang to dark matter."
>
> After: "The book covers the Big Bang and dark matter."

### Inflated copulas [performs]

`Serves as`, `stands as`, `functions as`, `acts as`, `represents`, `boasts`,
and `features` can inflate a simple `is` or `has`. Use the simple verb when
nothing else changes. Keep the stronger verb when it expresses a real function
or representation.

> Before: "Gallery 825 serves as the association's exhibition space and
> features four rooms."
>
> After: "Gallery 825 is the association's exhibition space and has four
> rooms."

### Obscured responsibility [evades]

Passive voice and abstract agency are problems when they hide who did
something the reader needs to evaluate. Name the actor only when the material
identifies one.

> Before: "After its review, the board reached a decision to approve the
> budget."
>
> After: "The board approved the budget after its review."

Do not mechanically replace ordinary metonymy such as "the data show" or "the
court held." Keep passive voice when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, obvious,
or conventionally backgrounded.

### Vague or inflated attribution [evades]

`Experts argue`, `studies show`, `critics say`, `widely regarded as`, or one
source described as several can borrow authority. Name the source and its
actual claim when available. Otherwise narrow the attribution, ask for the
source, or flag or cut the claim.

> Source: One supplied review calls the interface confusing; no other review is
> available.
>
> Before: "One review called the interface confusing, showing that reviewers
> found it difficult to use."
>
> After: "One review called the interface confusing."

### Hedge stacks [evades]

Several hedges on one proposition can make its uncertainty impossible to
interpret. Keep the one that most accurately calibrates the claim.

> Before: "The policy could potentially possibly affect response times."
>
> After: "The policy may affect response times."

### Unsupported universals [evades]

`Always`, `never`, `everyone`, and `nobody` overstate a claim when the material
supports only a tendency or example. Narrow the quantifier to the evidence.
Keep a universal the source can defend.

### Repeated question or discourse openers [flattens]

Several sentences beginning `What`, `Why`, or `How`, or several paragraphs
beginning `So`, `Look`, or the same transition, can become a template. Change
only the accidental repetition.

### Concession boilerplate [performs]

`Despite these challenges, X continues to thrive` often dismisses the named
problems with a generic positive turn. State the supported problems and
outcome without the ceremonial recovery.

## 4. Paragraphs and composition

### A bow on every paragraph [flattens]

Repeated miniature takeaways make a piece feel pre-resolved. Let a paragraph
end when its work is done. Do not enforce a numerical quota: some instructional
and persuasive genres need explicit takeaways.

### Announce, explain, recap [flattens]

Introductions, topic sentences, and summaries may repeat one point at three
scales. Remove the layer that contributes no orientation or synthesis. Keep
recaps needed for a long, technical, accessible, or decision-oriented document.

### Generic conclusions [performs]

`In conclusion`, `Overall`, `Ultimately`, and upbeat send-offs can restate the
piece without adding a decision, implication, or action. Cut empty closure.
Keep a conclusion when the genre expects synthesis and the paragraph actually
provides it.

### Template-shaped structure [flattens]

Generic headings such as `Introduction`, `Background`, `Challenges`, and
`Future outlook`, a heading over every few lines, or a warm-up sentence that
repeats its heading can reveal scaffolding rather than structure. Organize
around the reader's questions and the content's real divisions.

Do not rename required sections in academic, legal, policy, or organizational
templates.

### Synonym cycling [flattens]

Unnecessary synonyms can make one subject appear to be several. Repeat the
clearest term when consistency matters.

> Before: "The protagonist faces a choice. The central figure delays it. The
> hero eventually decides."
>
> After: "The protagonist delays the choice but eventually decides."

### Transition clustering [flattens]

Repeated sentence-initial `Furthermore`, `Moreover`, `Additionally`,
`Consequently`, `Subsequently`, `Notably`, or any replacement word can make
connections feel mechanical. Remove transitions the logic does not need and
vary the remaining connection only when the relationship changes. There is no
whole-document quota.

### Dilution [flattens]

Watch for one point repeated across sections, a metaphor extended after it has
done its work, or several analogies supplying borrowed authority. Consolidate
repetition while preserving distinctions and deliberate motifs.

### Change-log language in durable documentation [evades]

Docs and comments sometimes narrate what was added or replaced instead of
describing current behavior. Rewrite for the current reader. Preserve
change-oriented language in changelogs, release notes, migration guides, and
historical explanations.

## 5. Formatting

### Mechanical list styling [flattens]

Bold-label bullets, identical three-item lists, tiny tables, or a heading over
every paragraph may package weak content as structure. Choose prose, bullets,
or a table according to the relationship among the items. Keep established
house patterns and genuinely scannable lists.

### Decorative emphasis [performs]

Repeated boldface, emoji, symbols, or title-case headings can add energy
without information. Remove decoration that conflicts with the genre or house
style. Keep functional status symbols, requested emoji, and the publication's
heading convention.

### Dashes and punctuation [flattens]

An em dash is ordinary punctuation, not an authorship signal. Review it only
when repeated parenthetical asides or dramatic interruptions become a
mannerism, conflict with house style, or blur the sentence. Preserve the
writer's natural rate when it reads well.

An en dash serves different jobs, including ranges and relationships. Apply the
relevant style guide; do not count it with em dashes. Double hyphens may be
valid in source formats that convert them during rendering.

### Format leakage [evades]

Flag Markdown in a plain-text destination, skipped heading levels, broken list
nesting, raw template syntax, internal reference tokens, and decorative
characters that will not render as intended. Preserve literal syntax in code
and examples.

### Inconsistent quotation marks [flattens]

Mixed straight and curly marks may be accidental. Normalize them only when the
destination or house style requires it. Either style alone says nothing about
authorship.

## 6. Evidence and citations

### Vague declaratives [evades]

`The implications are significant`, `The reasons are structural`, and `The
stakes are high` announce a conclusion without giving it. Name the implication,
reason, or stake from the authorized material, or cut or flag the sentence.

### Unsupported specificity [evades]

Never repair vagueness by inventing a number, actor, mechanism, place, example,
or anecdote. Specificity improves prose only when it is true and relevant.

> Before: "The campaign faced financial challenges."
>
> Safe repair without more evidence: "The campaign had financial problems."
>
> Better repair: ask for or locate the authorized figure, then state it.

### Unsupported interpretation or causation [evades]

Facts placed next to one another can imply a cause, motive, trend, or symbolic
meaning the source never establishes. Separate the facts, qualify the
relationship, cite the interpretation, or flag it.

### Notability piling [performs]

Lists of publications, awards, clients, citations, followers, or
`independent coverage` can substitute volume for context. Keep the evidence
that matters to the claim. Do not trim a required bibliography or invent what
a source said.

### Citation integrity [evades]

Look for malformed references, impossible metadata, unresolved placeholders,
dead or suspicious links, missing locators where the genre requires them,
tracking parameters, and leaked internal citation tokens.

When verification is in scope, check each concern against authorized sources
and record uncertainty. When it is not, preserve the citation and link target
and flag the concern. Never silently fabricate or substitute a reference.

## 7. Tone

### Sycophancy and announcement theater [performs]

`Great question`, `You are absolutely right`, `We are thrilled to announce`,
and exclamation clusters can praise the reader or event instead of delivering
information. Lead with the answer or announcement. Keep warmth that fits the
speaker and relationship.

### Press-release voice [performs]

Commitments to excellence, innovation, sustainability, customer focus, or
community can conceal what an organization did. State the supported action,
target, result, or limitation. Preserve attributed language when reporting what
an organization said.

### Symmetrical neutrality [evades]

Perfectly balanced `on one hand` treatment can hide the conclusion the
material supports. State the warranted position and represent genuine
disagreement accurately. Do not manufacture certainty or erase material
counterevidence.

### Patronizing instruction [performs]

`Think of it as`, `Let us break this down`, and beginner metaphors can talk
below the intended reader. Teach at the level the audience needs.

### Unmarked coinages [performs]

A fresh label presented as established can manufacture authority. Describe the
idea plainly or introduce the coinage as the writer's shorthand and define it.

### Borrowed intimacy [performs]

`Trust me`, `I promise`, `We have all been there`, and invented personal
anecdotes claim a relationship or experience the material has not earned. Cut
them unless they are authentic to the writer and relevant.

## 8. Audit guidance

An Audit identifies observable choices and reader effects. It does not
determine whether a person or system wrote the text.

### Report material issues

Use this compact shape:

> **Excerpt or location:** brief enough to identify the passage
>
> **Pattern:** the catalog name or a plain description
>
> **Effect:** what it obscures, inflates, repeats, or disrupts here
>
> **Repair:** the smallest useful direction

Group repeated instances of the same pattern when a line-by-line list would be
noisy. Distinguish high-impact evidence or meaning defects from minor style
choices.

### Describe clusters without a verdict

A cluster can justify a broader editing pass when several independent problems
co-occur, such as unsupported importance, repetitive transitions, generic
structure, and vague attribution. Describe the cluster and its effect. Do not
turn it into an origin claim, probability, or accusation.

### Do not use these as provenance evidence

The following features may still be worth reporting when they materially harm
the prose or violate the genre. Alone, they do not support an authorship claim:

- polished grammar or clean formatting
- formal, academic, technical, or multilingual diction
- mixed registers in a multi-author or specialist document
- one transition, dash, triad, passive sentence, fragment, or rhetorical
  question
- blandness, verbosity, or missing citations
- a word that appears in a watch list

### Preserve voice evidence

Keep characteristic vocabulary, defensible certainty, honest hedges,
parentheticals, in-jokes, uneven rhythm, recurring syntax, and unusual concrete
detail when they serve the piece. These features are reasons for editing
restraint, not proof of who wrote the text.

If the caller needs provenance, recommend evidence from the writing process:
drafts, revision history, notes, source trails, and a conversation with the
writer. Style analysis may guide questions but cannot settle authorship by
itself.

## Evidence basis

The caution above reflects both the limits and the bounded value of style
analysis:

- The source taxonomy describes its indicators as descriptive rather than
  prescriptive and warns that their presence does not establish authorship:
  [Wikipedia, "Signs of AI writing," pinned
  revision](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing&oldid=1365519760).
- Published evaluations find that text-only detectors can produce substantial
  false positives and lose accuracy across populations, domains, generators,
  and edits:
  [Liang et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10382961/),
  [Weber-Wulff et
  al.](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40979-023-00146-z),
  [Dugan et al.](https://aclanthology.org/2024.acl-long.674/), and
  [Sadasivan et al.](https://openreview.net/forum?id=OOgsAZdFOt).
- A bounded study also found that experienced readers could combine lexical and
  structural cues effectively on its English nonfiction sample. That result
  supports careful review, not universal attribution:
  [Russell et al.](https://aclanthology.org/2025.acl-long.267/).
